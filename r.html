R-Based Analysis - Loyalty Trends for Sales Improvement

See full presentation in Storytelling.

Tasks:

  • Performed exploratory and statistical analysis using R to support client's preferred workflows.
  • Analysed customer behaviour, loyalty point distribution, and feature relationships.
  • Built and evaluated a Multiple Linear Regression (MLR) model to predict loyalty points.
  • Generated visual insights and made business-focused recommendations.

R Libraries Used:

  • tidyverse, dplyr, ggplot2, stringr, tidyr: Data cleaning, transformation, and visualisation
  • corrplot: Correlation matrix visualisation
  • car: Variance Inflation Factor (VIF) testing for multicollinearity
  • conflicted: Managing function conflicts between packages

Key Findings:

  • Loyalty points are right-skewed, non-normally distributed, with a wide range and moderate kurtosis (no extreme outliers).
  • Annual income and spending score showed the strongest predictive power for loyalty points (correlation coefficients: 0.62 and 0.67).
  • An adjusted R² of 83% indicates the MLR model (using income and spending score) explains loyalty points variance effectively.
  • Age was identified as a confounding variable (via VIF), introducing multicollinearity, and was excluded from the final model.
  • Spending and income data revealed customer segments with similar income but differing loyalty points, suggesting varying behaviour within the same income brackets.

Recommendations:

  • Prioritise spending score (SS) over income in loyalty point allocation to better reward high-spending, low-income customers.
  • Use regression and clustering insights to personalise marketing strategies and enhance customer retention.
  • Improve the loyalty programme by incorporating engagement-based metrics (e.g. purchases, reviews), tracked via unique customer IDs.
  • To strengthen predictive accuracy, consider expanding the dataset, refining outliers, and integrating redemption history of loyalty points for time-based engagement analysis.

The Rebound Effect: Fuel Efficiency and Motor Vehicle Travel (“Jevons paradox”):

Vehicle fuel efficiency is seen as a possible solution in reduction of gasoline usage and consequential reduction of harmful gas emissions.
Aimed smaller pollution nevertheless was met with a rebound effect: as miles per gallon ratio improved, vehicles owners were driving more as their fuel cost per mile travelled decreased. The study is based on 'panel data' across 50 US states.

The calculations were conducted in Excel and STATA.
